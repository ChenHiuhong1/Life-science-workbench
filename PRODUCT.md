# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Science Workbench is used by researchers, bioinformaticians, graduate students, and lab members working on local research projects. They are usually in a task-heavy desktop flow: chatting with an agent, searching literature, running Python or R analysis, reviewing generated artifacts, writing documents, or connecting to HPC resources.

## Product Purpose

Science Workbench is a local-first desktop AI workbench for research workflows. It brings LLM chat, literature search, study design, bioinformatics analysis, computational structural biology, wet-lab protocol support, document review, artifact tracking, project workspaces, and HPC operations into one Windows application while keeping user data on the local machine.

Success means the interface feels trustworthy enough for real scientific work, stays fast during long streams and agent switches, makes generated artifacts easy to inspect, and keeps project context visible without making the workspace feel crowded.

## Brand Personality

Warm, precise, elevated, intellectually sharp, and grounded in SYSU green.

The product should feel like a premium scientific instrument in a warm university research studio: warm ivory surfaces, Sun Yat-sen University green accents, deep green ink text, clear hierarchy, exact controls, and restrained material contrast. It should feel refined and welcoming without becoming decorative.

## Anti-references

Avoid flat beige washes, cheap gold gradients, decorative glass effects, oversized rounded cards, poetic microcopy, fake dashboard previews, excessive all-caps labels, and low-contrast gray text. Avoid visual changes that reduce information density, hide project state, or make standard product controls feel unfamiliar.

## Design Principles

1. Keep the work in focus. Navigation, project state, conversation, and artifacts should be visible without competing for attention.
2. Use premium restraint. The interface earns polish through typography, alignment, material contrast, and small state details rather than decoration.
3. Make scientific outputs inspectable. Figures, tables, code, reviews, and paths should be legible and quickly actionable.
4. Preserve flow under load. Streaming, long histories, and agent switches should feel steady and predictable.
5. Treat local trust as part of the UI. Project folders, saved settings, memory, and generated files should be surfaced with clarity.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA contrast for text and controls. Keep motion minimal and state-driven, with reduced-motion-safe behavior. Use familiar controls, clear focus states, and color-independent status cues for warnings, errors, success, and selections.
